Migraine Clinical Guidance

Overview
Migraine is a primary headache disorder characterized by recurrent moderate to severe headaches often associated with nausea, vomiting, and sensitivity to light and sound.

Common Clinical Features
Migraine headaches typically present with the following characteristics:

* Unilateral head pain (one side of head)
* Pulsating or throbbing quality
* Moderate to severe intensity
* Worsens with routine physical activity
* Duration between 4 and 72 hours

Associated Symptoms

Patients may experience one or more of the following symptoms:

* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Photophobia (light sensitivity)
* Phonophobia (sound sensitivity)
* Visual disturbances (aura in some patients)

Migraine with Aura

Aura symptoms can precede headache and may include:

* Visual flashes or zigzag lines
* Blind spots
* Tingling sensation in face or hands
* Speech disturbance

Aura typically lasts 5–60 minutes.

Diagnostic Considerations

Migraine is typically diagnosed clinically when a patient has:

* At least 5 headache episodes
* Headache lasting 4–72 hours
* At least two of the following:

  * Unilateral location
  * Pulsating quality
  * Moderate or severe pain intensity
  * Aggravation by routine physical activity

And at least one of:

* Nausea or vomiting
* Photophobia and phonophobia

Red Flags (Consider Imaging)

Neuroimaging should be considered if the patient has:

* Sudden onset "worst headache of life"
* New headache after age 50
* Neurological deficits
* Fever or stiff neck
* Head trauma
* Progressive worsening pattern

Acute Treatment

First-line treatment options include:

* NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
* Acetaminophen
* Triptans for moderate to severe migraine
* Anti-nausea medications (metoclopramide, prochlorperazine)

Preventive Therapy

Consider preventive treatment if:

* More than 4 migraine days per month
* Significant functional impairment

Preventive medications may include:

* Beta blockers (propranolol)
* Topiramate
* Amitriptyline
* CGRP inhibitors

Lifestyle and Trigger Management

Patients should be advised to identify and avoid migraine triggers:

* Sleep deprivation
* Stress
* Certain foods (aged cheese, alcohol)
* Dehydration
* Hormonal fluctuations

Patients may benefit from:

* Regular sleep schedule
* Adequate hydration
* Stress management techniques
