Cluster Headache Clinical Guidance

Overview
Cluster headache is a primary headache disorder characterized by severe unilateral pain occurring in cyclical patterns called cluster periods.

These headaches are considered among the most painful headache types.

Clinical Characteristics

Cluster headaches typically present with:

* Severe unilateral orbital, supraorbital, or temporal pain
* Sudden onset
* Pain lasting 15 to 180 minutes

Headaches may occur:

* Up to 8 times per day
* Often at the same time each day

Associated Autonomic Symptoms

Cluster headaches are associated with ipsilateral autonomic symptoms such as:

* Red eye (conjunctival injection)
* Tearing (lacrimation)
* Nasal congestion
* Runny nose
* Eyelid swelling
* Facial sweating
* Drooping eyelid (ptosis)

Behavior During Attacks

Unlike migraine, patients with cluster headache often appear restless or agitated and may pace during attacks.

Cluster Periods

Headaches occur in clusters lasting:

* Weeks to months
* Followed by remission periods

Risk Factors

Cluster headaches are more common in:

* Males
* Smokers
* Adults aged 20–50

Triggers

During cluster periods, attacks may be triggered by:

* Alcohol
* Strong odors
* Nitroglycerin

Evaluation

Neuroimaging should be considered to exclude secondary causes, especially if symptoms are atypical.

Acute Treatment

First-line treatments include:

* High-flow oxygen therapy
* Subcutaneous sumatriptan
* Intranasal triptans

Preventive Treatment

Preventive therapy during cluster periods may include:

* Verapamil
* Corticosteroids (short-term)
* Lithium (in chronic cases)

Referral

Patients with suspected cluster headache should often be referred to a neurologist or headache specialist.
